Five paintings everyone half-remembers, looked at properly.

A small single-page exhibition of five famous works, each paired with a short note on what it's usually understood to mean.

On the Wall

5 works · public domain
Mona Lisa
Leonardo da Vinci · c.1503 · oil on poplar

Best known for its ambiguous, faint smile and the way her gaze seems to follow the viewer. Often read as an early study of psychological depth — a portrait less about status and more about an unreadable inner life.

The Starry Night
Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · oil on canvas

Painted from memory while van Gogh was in an asylum, the swirling sky is usually read as turmoil and restlessness pressing in around a quiet, sleeping village — turbulence held just above ordinary life.

The Scream
Edvard Munch · 1893 · tempera and pastel on cardboard

The wavy sky and distorted figure are widely read as an externalized panic attack — Munch described feeling a "scream through nature," making the landscape itself feel like it's reacting to the figure's anxiety.

Girl with a Pearl Earring
Johannes Vermeer · 1665 · oil on canvas

Sometimes called the "Mona Lisa of the North," it isn't a portrait of a named person but a "tronie" — a study of an expression. The half-turned glance gives it an intimacy that feels caught mid-moment rather than posed.

Guernica
Pablo Picasso · 1937 · oil on canvas

Painted in response to the bombing of the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War, its greyscale chaos of screaming figures and broken bodies is one of the most direct anti-war statements in modern art.
